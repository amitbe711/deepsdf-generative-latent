\title{\textbf{Generative Latent Models for DeepSDF:\\
Gaussian vs.\ Diffusion in the Small-Data Regime}}
\author{Amit Benbenishti \\ Deep Learning for 3D Computer Vision, Hebrew University of Jerusalem}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
DeepSDF~\citep{park2019deepsdf} represents a shape category with one MLP and a
per-shape latent code, but it does not learn a \emph{distribution} over those
codes, so drawing new codes at random rarely decodes to a valid shape. Latent
diffusion (3D-LDM~\citep{nam2022lion3dldm}) fixes this at full ShapeNet scale,
but its benefit over a trivial Gaussian prior in the low-data, low-capacity
regime is unstudied. We train a DeepSDF auto-decoder, freeze it, and fit two
generators over the resulting codes---a multivariate Gaussian and a DDPM
denoising the codes directly---and compare them on a controlled grid of dataset
sizes $N \in \{10,50,150\}$ and latent dimensions $D \in \{16,32\}$. We evaluate
reconstruction (Chamfer, IoU) and generation (Coverage, MMD, 1-NN accuracy).
Our study characterizes exactly when the added complexity of diffusion pays off:
at very small $N$ the Gaussian is competitive with---and often
indistinguishable from---the DDPM, consistent with a negative result for
diffusion in the minimal-resource setting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Related Work}

\paragraph{Implicit shape representations.}
A signed distance function (SDF) represents a surface implicitly as the zero
level set of $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$, where $f(\mathbf{x})$ is the signed
distance from $\mathbf{x}$ to the surface (negative inside). DeepSDF
\citep{park2019deepsdf} learns a \emph{single} MLP $f_\theta(\mathbf{z},
\mathbf{x})$ shared across a shape category, conditioning on a per-shape latent
code $\mathbf{z}$; the surface for code $\mathbf{z}$ is $\{\mathbf{x} :
f_\theta(\mathbf{z}, \mathbf{x}) = 0\}$, extracted with Marching Cubes
\citep{lorensen1987marchingcubes}. It is trained as an \emph{auto-decoder}: the
codes are free parameters optimized jointly with $\theta$. Occupancy Networks
\citep{mescheder2019occnet} and IM-Net \citep{chen2019imnet} are contemporaneous
implicit representations, and periodic-activation networks
(SIREN~\citep{sitzmann2020siren}) improve the fitting of such fields.

\paragraph{Generation over latent codes.}
DeepSDF's L2 code regularizer is the MAP estimate under a zero-mean Gaussian
prior, so in principle one could sample $\mathbf{z}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ and
decode. In practice the learned codes do not fill an isotropic Gaussian and
naive sampling yields broken shapes. 3D-LDM \citep{nam2022lion3dldm} instead
trains a \emph{latent diffusion model} over DeepSDF codes at full ShapeNet
scale, following DDPM \citep{ho2020ddpm}; diffusion also underlies 3D generation
via score distillation (DreamFusion \citep{poole2022dreamfusion}, our HW3).
Alternative small-data shape generators include shape VAEs with geometric
augmentation (GLASS~\citep{muralikrishnan2021glass}) and rigidity-regularized
auto-decoders (ARAPReg~\citep{huang2021arapreg}).

\paragraph{Our question and contribution.}
All of the above operate at scale. We ask a controlled, resource-conscious
question that the literature leaves open: \emph{in the small-$N$, small-$D$
regime, does a DDPM over DeepSDF codes actually beat a plain Gaussian?} We keep
Stage~1 fixed and vary only the Stage~2 generator, sweeping $N$ and $D$, and
report both reconstruction and distribution-level generation metrics
\citep{achlioptas2018latentpc}. A rigorous negative result (Gaussian $\approx$
diffusion at small $N$) is a valid and informative outcome. All training code is
our own (see the code README and \texttt{src/thirdparty/}).

\section{Method}

\subsection{Stage 1: DeepSDF auto-decoder}
We assign each of the $N$ training shapes a latent code $\mathbf{z}_i \in
\mathbb{R}^D$, initialized from $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2)$, and share a decoder
$f_\theta$. The decoder is an 8-layer MLP of width 512 with ReLU activations, a
skip connection that re-injects the input $[\mathbf{z};\mathbf{x}]$ at layer~4,
weight normalization, and a $\tanh$ on the scalar output. We initialize it with
\emph{geometric initialization} \citep{atzmon2020sal, gropp2020igr} so that the
network starts as an approximate sphere SDF; without this we observed the
decoder collapsing to a spatially-constant field for some $(N,D)$ cells (no zero
crossing, hence no extractable surface).

\paragraph{Sampling supervision.} For each normalized mesh (centered, scaled
into the unit sphere) we sample points with $90\%$ near the surface (surface
points perturbed by two Gaussians, $\sigma\in\{0.005, 0.02\}$) and $10\%$
uniformly in $[-1,1]^3$, and record their signed distances.

\paragraph{Loss.} Following DeepSDF we minimize a clamped-$L_1$ reconstruction
loss plus code regularization:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{|B|}\sum_{(i,\mathbf{x})\in B}
\bigl| \mathrm{clamp}(f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{x}), \delta)
     - \mathrm{clamp}(s(\mathbf{x}), \delta) \bigr|
\;+\; \lambda \, \frac{1}{|B|}\sum_i \lVert \mathbf{z}_i \rVert_2^2,
\end{equation}
where $s(\mathbf{x})$ is the ground-truth SDF, $\delta=0.1$, and
$\lambda=10^{-4}$. The decoder weights $\theta$ and the codes
$\{\mathbf{z}_i\}$ are optimized jointly with Adam.

\subsection{Stage 2: generative priors over frozen codes}
We freeze $f_\theta$ and $\{\mathbf{z}_i\}$ and fit two generators.

\paragraph{Gaussian (minimal).} We fit a multivariate Gaussian
$\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$ to the codes by maximum likelihood
(full covariance with a small ridge, or a diagonal variant) and sample
$\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + L\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ with $\Sigma=LL^\top$.

\paragraph{Latent DDPM (expressive).} We train a denoising diffusion model
directly on the $D$-dimensional codes, reusing the noise-prediction objective of
HW3 \citep{ho2020ddpm}. With a cosine schedule
\citep{nichol2021improvedddpm} $\{\bar\alpha_t\}$, the forward process is
$\mathbf{z}_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{z}_0 +
\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$, and a small MLP
$\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\phi(\mathbf{z}_t, t)$ (sinusoidal time embedding,
residual blocks) is trained with
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{DDPM}} = \mathbb{E}_{t,\boldsymbol\epsilon}
\lVert \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_\phi(\mathbf{z}_t, t) - \boldsymbol{\epsilon}
\rVert_2^2$. We generate by ancestral sampling from $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$.

\subsection{Inference and meshing}
To realize a sampled code we evaluate $f_\theta(\mathbf{z}, \cdot)$ on a dense
grid over $[-1,1]^3$ and run Marching Cubes at level 0 to obtain a mesh, from
which we sample a surface point cloud for the metrics.

\section{Evaluation and Results}

\subsection{Setup}
We sweep $N \in \{10, 50, 150\}$ and $D \in \{16, 32\}$; for each cell we train
Stage~1, then both generators. The default data are analytic parametric
``chairs'' (a coherent shape category with an exact SDF, so results are fully
reproducible and preprocessing-free); the identical pipeline runs on a ShapeNet
chairs subset by pointing \texttt{data.source} at a mesh directory. Full
hyper-parameters are in \texttt{configs/} and reproduced by
\texttt{scripts/run\_grid.py}.

\subsection{Metrics}
\textbf{Reconstruction:} Chamfer distance and volumetric IoU between each decoded
training code and its ground truth. \textbf{Generation}
\citep{achlioptas2018latentpc}, computed between a set of generated shapes and a
held-out reference set of real shapes, using Chamfer distance between surface
point clouds: \emph{Coverage} (fraction of references matched, measures
diversity/mode coverage), \emph{MMD} (minimum matching distance, measures
fidelity), and \emph{1-NN accuracy} (a two-sample test; $0.5$ is ideal,
indicating the generated and real distributions are indistinguishable).

\subsection{Discussion}
The results support our hypothesis: in the small-data regime the \emph{marginal}
value of diffusion over a Gaussian is small, and often negative. Reconstruction
is strong and roughly flat across the grid (Chamfer $\approx 1.0$--$1.5\times
10^{-3}$, IoU $0.68$--$0.80$; Figure~\ref{fig:recon}), confirming that Stage~1
has enough capacity at this scale, so differences between generators reflect the
Stage~2 prior rather than the decoder.

At $N=10$ and $N=50$ the Gaussian matches or beats the DDPM on the two-sample
1-NN test (closer to the ideal $0.5$): at $N{=}50,\,D{=}16$ the Gaussian scores
$0.625$ versus the DDPM's $0.750$, and at $N{=}50,\,D{=}32$ it scores $0.662$
versus $0.875$; the Gaussian also attains equal or higher Coverage in every
small-$N$ cell. Intuitively, with only a handful of codes the empirical latent
distribution is nearly Gaussian and the DDPM has little structure to exploit,
while its extra parameters are harder to fit from few examples.

The crossover appears only at the largest $N$: at $N{=}150,\,D{=}16$ the DDPM
finally edges the Gaussian on 1-NN accuracy ($0.637$ vs.\ $0.688$) and MMD
($2.09$ vs.\ $2.19\times10^{-3}$). The 1-NN curve for the DDPM at $D{=}16$
(Figure~\ref{fig:degradation}, right) decreases monotonically in $N$, whereas
the Gaussian is essentially flat---exactly the behavior expected if diffusion
needs data to express a non-Gaussian code distribution. The practical takeaway
for a Colab-scale budget is that a Gaussian prior is the better default until the
code set is large enough (here, $N\gtrsim 150$ at the smaller $D$) to reward the
diffusion model. Finally, an implementation finding independent of the
generator: geometric initialization was necessary for stable auto-decoder
training---plain initialization collapsed some $(N,D)$ cells to a
spatially-constant field with no extractable surface.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a controlled comparison of a Gaussian prior and a latent DDPM over
frozen DeepSDF codes in the small-data, small-capacity regime. Rather than
pursuing state of the art, we asked when diffusion is worth its added
complexity, and found that at very small $N$ a simple Gaussian is a strong,
often statistically indistinguishable baseline---a useful negative result for
practitioners working under Colab-scale budgets. Limitations include the use of
a synthetic shape category for the primary sweep (the pipeline supports
ShapeNet meshes) and modest decoder capacity; future work could scale $N$ and
$D$ further and add flow- or VAE-based priors as intermediate points on the
minimal-to-expressive spectrum.
